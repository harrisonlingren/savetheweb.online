Fight for Net Neutrality

This could be your last chance to stop ISPs from messing up your Internet.

The FCC is about to announce a vote to slash net neutrality rules, allowing ISPs like Verizon and Comcast to block apps, slow websites, and charge fees to control what you see & do online. Once they announce the vote, it's hard to stop. But if we flood them with calls and messages now, Congress can stop the vote.

Learn more at battleforthenet.com

Contact Congress in 5 minutes with ResistBot

ResistBot is an easy way to contact your local representatives about issues that matter in just 5 minutes. Here's the steps you need to take to contact your representatives about Net Neutrality:

  1. Text "resist" to 50409
  2. The bot will ask for your name. Text back with your first and last name.
  3. Next, your local address. Respond with your street address + ZIP code (1234 Main Street 43092)
  4. Once your address is confirmed, it will share the names of your representatives. Text "congress" to send a message to all of your representatives.
  5. Next it will ask for the message you want to send. Here's a brief message in text-sized chunks that you can copy-and-paste and send:

    Copy Net Neutrality is the cornerstone of innovation, free speech and democracy on the Internet.

    Copy Control over the Internet should remain in the hands of the people who use it every day.

    Copy The ability to share information without impediment is critical to the progression of technology, science, small business, and culture.

    Copy Please stand with the public by upholding the FCC's Title II Net Neutrality protections once and for all.

  6. Once the above has been sent, text "done" to confirm your message.
  7. Finally, once the message has been confirmed, text "send" to finish and send your message or text "preview" to make sure the bot saved it correctly.
Once it's been sent, you're good to go! I would recommend sending messages as often as you can - the easiest way for average Americans to influence their legislators is to make their phones ring off the hook. Interns and staffers make notes of the viewpoints of constituents who call in and pass that information along, and internal tallies of the positions taken in those calls do affect their decisions. The more calls and the more lopsided the tally, the more likely senators are to be influenced.
